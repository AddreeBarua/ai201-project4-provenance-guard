# Provenance Guard

A backend API that classifies whether creative writing was authored by a human or generated by AI. Built for any creative sharing platform that wants to surface transparent attribution labels to readers while giving creators a fair path to appeal.

---

## Architecture Overview

A submitted piece of text travels through the following path:

1. **POST /submit** receives the text and creator ID
2. **Signal 1 (Groq LLM)** analyzes semantic patterns and returns a score 0.0–1.0
3. **Signal 2 (Stylometric Heuristics)** measures sentence variance, vocabulary diversity, and punctuation density and returns a score 0.0–1.0
4. **Confidence Scoring** combines both signals into a weighted average (LLM 60%, Stylometric 40%)
5. **Transparency Label** maps the score to plain-English text shown to the reader
6. **Audit Log** records the full decision with all signal scores and timestamp
7. **JSON Response** returns content_id, attribution, confidence, both signal scores, and label text

For appeals: **POST /appeal** receives content_id and creator reasoning → updates status to "under_review" → appends appeal to audit log → returns confirmation.

---

## Detection Signals

### Signal 1: LLM-Based Classification (Groq)
- **What it measures:** Whether the text reads as AI-generated or human-written based on semantic patterns, word choice, and stylistic coherence
- **Why I chose it:** A language model can recognize holistic patterns — overly polished phrasing, uniform tone, repeated transitional phrases — that are hard to capture with simple statistics
- **What it misses:** Formal or academic human writing may score high. Non-native English speakers who write in structured ways may be misclassified.

### Signal 2: Stylometric Heuristics
- **What it measures:** Three statistical properties — sentence length variance, type-token ratio (vocabulary diversity), and punctuation density
- **Why I chose it:** AI text tends to be structurally uniform. Human writing is messier — sentence lengths vary, vocabulary is more diverse, punctuation is less predictable. This signal captures structure, not semantics, making it genuinely independent from Signal 1.
- **What it misses:** Formal human writing (legal documents, academic papers) may appear uniform and score high. Very short texts (under 50 words) don't provide enough data to measure variance reliably.

---

## Confidence Scoring

Both signals are combined into a single weighted score:

- **LLM score: 60% weight** (more reliable — captures semantic and stylistic coherence)
- **Stylometric score: 40% weight** (structural signal — less reliable on short texts)

**Score ranges:**
- 0.0 – 0.39 → Likely human-written
- 0.40 – 0.69 → Uncertain
- 0.70 – 1.0 → Likely AI-generated

**How I validated scores are meaningful:**

| Input | LLM Score | Stylo Score | Confidence | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Formal AI-sounding paragraph | 0.8 | 0.183 | 0.553 | uncertain |
| Casual human ramen review | 0.0 | 0.273 | 0.109 | likely_human |
| Formal academic human writing | 0.2 | 0.597 | 0.359 | likely_human |

The scores vary meaningfully across inputs. The casual human text scored 0.109 while the AI-sounding paragraph scored 0.553 — a clear separation. The formal academic text scored 0.359, correctly landing in the human range despite its structured style.

## Transparency Label

All three label variants shown to users:

**High-Confidence AI (score ≥ 0.70):**
> "Our system found strong indicators that this content may have been AI-generated. This does not mean the work has no value, but readers should be aware it may not reflect the author's original voice. If you created this yourself, you can submit an appeal below."

**Uncertain (score 0.40 – 0.69):**
> "Our system was unable to determine with confidence whether this content was written by a human or generated by AI. It may reflect a mix of both, or a writing style our system hasn't seen before. Take this label as a signal to look closer, not a final verdict."

**High-Confidence Human (score < 0.40):**
> "Our system found strong indicators that this content was written by a human. This assessment is based on writing style and structure — it is not a guarantee, but we are reasonably confident this reflects original human authorship."

## Rate Limiting

**Limits:** 10 requests per minute, 100 requests per day per IP address.

**Reasoning:** A legitimate creator submitting their own work would rarely need more than a few submissions per minute — writing and editing is slow. The 10/minute limit stops automated scripts from flooding the system while giving real users plenty of headroom. The 100/day limit prevents sustained abuse while allowing a creator to submit many drafts over a full day of work.

## Known Limitations

**Non-native English speakers:** A human writer whose second language is English may write in a formal, structured way that both signals flag as AI-generated. The LLM signal sees polished phrasing; the stylometric signal sees low variance. This is a known false positive risk tied directly to both signals' blind spots — neither can distinguish "formal because AI" from "formal because learned writing style."

**Short texts:** Poems, haikus, and other short-form writing (under 50 words) do not give the stylometric signal enough data to measure variance reliably. The system returns a neutral 0.5 for the stylometric signal on very short texts, which reduces confidence in both directions.

---

## Spec Reflection

**One way the spec helped:** Writing out the three label variants in planning.md before touching any code forced me to think about what 0.6 actually means to a non-technical reader. That decision — that 0.6 should feel uncertain, not "probably AI" — shaped the threshold design (0.70 for AI, not 0.50).

**One way implementation diverged:** The spec assumed stylometric heuristics would clearly separate AI from human text. In practice, the stylometric signal alone was a weak separator — formal human writing scored similarly to AI writing. The LLM signal ended up doing most of the heavy lifting, which is why I weighted it 60/40 rather than 50/50 as originally planned.
