# Planning: Provenance Guard

## Detection Signals

### Signal 1: LLM-Based Classification (Groq)
- **What it measures:** Whether the text reads as AI-generated or human-written based on semantic patterns, word choice, and stylistic coherence
- **Why it works:** AI-generated text tends to be overly polished, uniform in tone, and uses certain phrases repeatedly. A language model can recognize these patterns holistically.
- **Output:** A score between 0.0 and 1.0 (0.0 = definitely human, 1.0 = definitely AI)
- **What it misses:** It can be fooled by humans who write formally or academically. It may also misclassify non-native English speakers whose writing appears more structured.

### Signal 2: Stylometric Heuristics
- **What it measures:** Statistical properties of the text — sentence length variance, vocabulary diversity (type-token ratio), and punctuation density
- **Why it works:** AI text tends to be uniform — similar sentence lengths, consistent vocabulary, predictable punctuation. Human writing is messier and more variable.
- **Output:** A score between 0.0 and 1.0 (0.0 = very human-like variability, 1.0 = very AI-like uniformity)
- **What it misses:** Formal human writing (academic papers, legal documents) may score high even though it's human-written. Short texts don't have enough data to measure variability reliably.

## Uncertainty Representation

- **Combined score:** The final confidence score is a weighted average of both signals:
  - LLM score: 60% weight (more reliable, semantic understanding)
  - Stylometric score: 40% weight (structural, but less reliable on short texts)

- **What the score means:**
  - 0.0 – 0.39 → Likely human-written
  - 0.40 – 0.69 → Uncertain (could be either)
  - 0.70 – 1.0 → Likely AI-generated

- **What 0.6 means:** The system leans toward AI but is not confident — the label will reflect that uncertainty and encourage the reader not to draw strong conclusions.

- **Design principle:** We deliberately set a high threshold (0.70) before calling something AI-generated, because a false positive (wrongly labeling a human's work as AI) is worse than a false negative on a creative writing platform.

## Transparency Label Variants

### High-Confidence AI (score ≥ 0.70)
> "Our system found strong indicators that this content may have been AI-generated. 
> This does not mean the work has no value, but readers should be aware it may not 
> reflect the author's original voice. If you created this yourself, you can submit 
> an appeal below."

### Uncertain (score 0.40 – 0.69)
> "Our system was unable to determine with confidence whether this content was 
> written by a human or generated by AI. It may reflect a mix of both, or a 
> writing style our system hasn't seen before. Take this label as a signal to 
> look closer, not a final verdict."

### High-Confidence Human (score < 0.40)
> "Our system found strong indicators that this content was written by a human. 
> This assessment is based on writing style and structure — it is not a guarantee, 
> but we are reasonably confident this reflects original human authorship."

## Appeals Workflow

- **Who can appeal:** Any creator who submitted content and received a classification they disagree with
- **What they provide:** Their content_id and a written explanation of why they believe the classification is wrong
- **What the system does:**
  - Updates the content status from "classified" to "under_review"
  - Logs the appeal alongside the original classification in the audit log
  - Returns a confirmation message to the creator
- **What a human reviewer sees:** The original text, the confidence score, both signal scores, and the creator's reasoning — all in one audit log entry

## Anticipated Edge Cases

1. **Non-native English speakers:** A human writer whose second language is English may write in a formal, structured way that both signals flag as AI-generated. The stylometric signal will see low variance; the LLM signal may see polished phrasing. This is a known false positive risk.

2. **Short poems or haikus:** Very short texts (under 50 words) don't give the stylometric signal enough data to measure variance reliably. A 3-line poem could score anywhere regardless of origin. The system should flag short texts as inherently uncertain.
